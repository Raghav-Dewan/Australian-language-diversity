The Uneven Geography of Language

Australia's capital city regions vary dramatically. Greater Sydney leads at 39.3% - nearly four times the rate of Greater Hobart (9.9%).

Use the threshold slider below the map to isolate only the most multilingual states. Scroll to zoom and drag to pan across Australia.

This east-coast concentration reflects the pull of skilled migration pathways, international student enrolments, and established diaspora networks in Australia's three largest cities.

Three Cities Carry the Load

In absolute numbers the concentration is even starker. Sydney's 1.86 million and Melbourne's 1.58 million together account for nearly 70% of all non-English speakers in Australian capital cities.

Circle size encodes total speakers; colour encodes the share of each city's total population. Hover any circle for exact figures.

Darwin is small in absolute size but high in share (27.7%) : reflecting its geographical proximity to South-East Asia and its role as a regional gateway city.

Not All Capitals Are Equal

The gap between Sydney (39.3%) and Hobart (9.9%) is vast - yet every city is growing more diverse. Use the slider to filter; click the legend to isolate a city.

Every City Is Growing More Diverse

Between 2016 and 2021, every single Australian capital city grew more linguistically diverse. The steepest slopes belong to Brisbane and Perth, both driven by South Asian arrivals. Even Greater Hobart added 1.7 percentage points. The trend is unambiguous: Australia is becoming more multilingual everywhere.

Which Languages Are Growing and Which Are Fading?

The 2021 Census captured a generational shift. South Asian languages surged. European languages of the post-war era declined. Hover for 2016 vs 2021 detail.

Punjabi's 80% Surge - and Italian's Slow Fade

The standout story is Punjabi's 80.4% growth. Meanwhile Italian fell 11.9% and Greek fell 7.9%. These numbers represent a profound intergenerational transition: the languages of Australia's post-war migration era are giving way to the languages of South Asia, South-East Asia, and the Middle East.

Each City Has Its Own Linguistic Fingerprint

The same languages appear everywhere, but their relative weight varies strikingly. Arabic concentrates in Sydney; Vietnamese shapes Melbourne; Punjabi defines Perth.

Perth is distinct: Punjabi is its second language rather than Vietnamese or Cantonese, reflecting WA's unique migration corridor from the Indian subcontinent.

Language Diversity and Socioeconomic Disadvantage: A Complex Picture

The dashed regression trend line shows a general negative relationship between multilingual share and SEIFA score. But the scatter reveals critical exceptions: Chatswood and Glen Waverley are highly multilingual and highly advantaged, showing that linguistic diversity does not equal disadvantage. Suburbs like Springvale and Cabramatta reflect communities where recent arrivals cluster in affordable areas. Language and place together reveal far more than either alone.

Methodology & Design Audit

1  Choropleth Map : Multilingual Share by State

Data: ABS Census 2021, LANP (Language Used at Home), aggregated to GCCSA then joined to GeoJSON by STATE_NAME. GeoJSON: tonywr71/GeoJson-Data (confirmed 8 features, STATE_NAME property).

Design: Sequential orangered colour scale encodes ordered quantitative share using luminance - the appropriate channel for this data type (Munzner Ch.10). Mercator projection centred [133, −28] provides a familiar view. Pan/zoom via Vega signal params provides exploration without separate controls.

WHAT: State boundaries (spatial) · Non-English share (quantitative)
WHY: Identify geographic distribution · compare states
HOW: Geoshape mark · Luminance channel · Threshold slider param · Text annotation layer
2  Proportional Symbol Map : Speaker Counts by City

Data: Speaker totals computed from GCCSA population × non-English share. Latitude/longitude: city centroids (verified against ABS GCCSA boundaries).

Design: Area (size channel) encodes magnitude - the correct choice when comparing values spanning 77× range (Sydney vs Hobart). Luminance reinforces the same quantity as a redundant channel. Threshold size scale prevents the largest circles from dominating. Layered over geoshape for geographic context.

WHAT: City centroids (spatial) · Speaker count (quantitative)
WHY: Compare absolute community sizes · locate concentration
HOW: Circle mark · Area channel · Luminance channel · Layered geoshape base
3  Dot Plot : Cities Ranked by Non-English Share

Design: Dot plot over bar chart: when values cluster between 10–40%, a bar chart's zero baseline wastes most of the chart area and exaggerates absolute differences. A dot plot with connecting rules preserves reading accuracy while eliminating baseline noise. Range slider param enables interactive filtering without a separate dropdown.

WHAT: Cities (nominal) · Share (quantitative)
WHY: Rank cities on shared scale · filter
HOW: Point mark · Position channel · Rule mark anchor · Range slider param · Inline text annotations
4  Change Bars : 2016 → 2021

Design: A zero-centred change bar chart makes gains and losses immediately legible. The chart now computes 2016 and 2021 shares directly from the census-by-year file, then derives a change value per city. Clicking the legend isolates a city while keeping the shared zero baseline visible.

WHAT: Cities (nominal) · 2016/2021 share (quantitative) · Change (quantitative)
WHY: Show directional change · compare rates of change
HOW: Zero baseline · Bar mark · Point endcaps · Legend binding param · Inline value labels
5–6  Bar Chart & Diverging Bar : Language Rankings & Growth

Design: Sorted horizontal bar encodes magnitude using position, the most accurate quantitative channel (Cleveland & McGill, 1984). Inline text labels remove the need to cross-reference the axis. The diverging bar uses hue (red/blue) to encode sign at a meaningful zero crossing, the appropriate channel for this data type. Conditional colour test separates categories without a separate field.

WHAT: Languages (nominal) · Speaker count / Change % (quantitative)
WHY: Compare community sizes · identify growing vs declining
HOW: Bar marks · Position channel · Conditional hue for sign · Checkbox param · Annotated reference values
7–8  Heatmap & Language Lines : City × Language Patterns

Design: The heatmap uses rect marks with luminance encoding across city × language to show where each language is strongest. The companion chart now uses a normalised stacked bar so readers can isolate a language directly from the legend while still comparing the whole city mix.

WHAT: City × language matrix (nominal) · Speaker count (quantitative)
WHY: Show linguistic fingerprint per city · compare composition
HOW: Rect mark (heatmap) · Luminance channel · Normalised stacked bars · Legend binding (composition chart)
9  Small Multiples : Top Languages per City

Design: Vega-Lite facet operator generates one bar panel per city with consistent axes. This enables city-to-city comparison without the visual clutter of 35 bars in a single chart. Consistent colour encoding (same language = same colour in all 12 visualisations) reduces cognitive load across the page.

WHAT: Language (nominal) · Speakers (quantitative) · City (nominal facet)
WHY: Compare city-specific language rankings · identify unique patterns
HOW: Facet operator · Bar marks · Consistent shared colour domain
10  Interactive Language Lines : Legend Binding

Design: A layered line-and-point chart shows how each language is distributed across the capital cities. The legend remains clickable so readers can isolate one language at a time, while the opacity condition preserves the rest of the context in the background.

WHAT: Language (nominal) · City (nominal) · Speakers (quantitative)
WHY: Trace where each language lives · compare city-level patterns
HOW: Line + point marks · Legend binding param · Opacity condition
11  Scatter + Brush : Multilingualism & SEIFA

Data: ABS SEIFA 2021 IRSAD scores, SA2 level. Language share: ABS Census 2021 data by region, cross-referenced for suburb-level examples across five cities.

Design: Scatter plot uses position (x, y), the most accurate quantitative channel for correlation tasks. A dashed regression line communicates the overall trend while an interval brush lets the reader focus on a cluster. Named suburbs remain highlighted in orange so the key outliers stay visible even when the rest of the points are filtered.

WHAT: Suburbs (nominal) · Multilingual % (quantitative) · SEIFA score (quantitative) · City (nominal)
WHY: Discover correlation · identify advantaged and disadvantaged outliers
HOW: Point marks · Position (x,y) · Regression transform · Dashed line mark · Interval brush param · Named suburb text + point annotations
